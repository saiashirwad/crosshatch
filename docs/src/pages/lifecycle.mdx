# Lifecycle

x402 exchanges move through three portable units: a payment requirement, a
payment payload, and facilitator verification or settlement.

## Atomic Units

x402 composes three independent pieces:

- Payment required: the merchant's description of what must be paid.
- Payment payload: the client's selected means of payment, usually including a
  signed transfer authorization.
- Facilitator service: the verification and settlement surface.

The merchant describes a payable resource. The client decides how to pay. The
facilitator checks the payment and connects it to settlement infrastructure.

## Payment Required

The lifecycle begins when a merchant determines that a requested resource
requires payment. Instead of returning the resource immediately, the merchant
returns a payment required. This could describe payment for the one resource,
multiple resources, a realtime session, a deposit... it's entirely up to the
merchant when and how to require payment.

The requirement acts like an invoice. It describes the resource and the accepted
ways to pay. A single requirement response can offer multiple options, allowing
the client to select one it can satisfy.

## Payment Payload

After reading the payment required, the client chooses one acceptable payment
mechanism and produces a payment payload.

The payload is the client's response to the invoice. It references the accepted
requirement entry and carries the concrete payment data for the selected scheme,
often a signed transfer authorization.

The payload may also carry extension data requested by the merchant. This lets
the client submit the means of payment and the merchant-specific context needed
to interpret, trace, or replay the exchange as one coherent unit.

## Facilitator Settlement

The facilitator service verifies that a submitted payment payload satisfies the
selected requirement and connects that verification step to settlement.

Depending on the scheme, the facilitator may validate signatures, check timeout
windows, prevent replay, submit a transfer, or confirm that settlement has
already occurred. The merchant uses that result to decide whether to deliver the
resource.

## Transport Agnosticism

x402 does not require HTTP even though HTTP `402 Payment Required` is the most
obvious deployment shape. Any transport can carry the requirement from merchant
to client and the payload back to the merchant or facilitator.

## Resource Delivery

The merchant can decide when to deliver the paid resource. It could for example
fork a settlement fiber and eagerly deliver the resource. Or it could block on
settlement, and only deliver the resource upon confirmation.

Delivery may happen as the response to a retried HTTP request, as a socket
message, as an out-of-band fulfillment event, or through any other channel the
program uses.
