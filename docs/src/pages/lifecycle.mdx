# Lifecycle

x402 is not a single request pattern. It is a small set of composable atomic units that can be carried across any
transport or service boundary.

This page outlines the lifecycle of an x402 exchange in terms of those units: the payment requirement, the payment
payload, and the facilitator service that verifies and/or settles payment.

## Atomic Units

x402 composes three independent pieces:

- Payment required: the merchant's description of what must be paid.
- Payment payload: the client's selected means of payment, usually including a signed transfer authorization.
- Facilitator service: the verification and settlement surface.

These units are intentionally separable. A merchant can construct requirements without knowing which wallet or account a
client will use. A client can create a payload after choosing one acceptable payment mechanism from the merchant's list.
A facilitator can verify and/or settle the selected payload without owning the merchant's application flow.

That separation is what makes x402 composable. The merchant describes a payable resource. The client decides how to pay.
The facilitator checks the payment and interacts with the relevant low-level settlement infrastructure.

## Payment Required

The lifecycle begins when a merchant determines that a requested resource requires payment. Instead of returning the
resource immediately, the merchant returns a payment required. This could describe payment for the one resource,
multiple resources, a realtime session, a deposit... it's entirely up to the merchant when and how to require payment.

The required acts like an invoice. It describes the resource, the accepted schemes, networks, assets, recipients,
amounts, timeouts, and any chain-specific metadata needed to create a valid payment payload. A single requirement
response can offer multiple acceptable ways to pay, allowing the client to select the option it can satisfy.

The important property is that the requirement is data. It is not tied to HTTP, a browser wallet, or a particular
runtime. It is the merchant's portable statement: "this resource can be delivered if one of these payment conditions is
met."

## Payment Payload

After reading the payment required, the client chooses one acceptable payment mechanism and produces a payment payload.

The payload is the client's response to the invoice. It references the accepted requirement entry and carries the
concrete payment data for the selected scheme. In many stablecoin flows, that concrete data is a signed transfer
authorization, such as an authorization that lets the recipient or facilitator execute a token transfer under the
constraints described by the requirement.

The payload may also carry extension data requested by the merchant. This lets the client submit the means of payment
and the merchant-specific context needed to interpret, trace, or replay the exchange as one coherent unit.

## Facilitator Settlement

The facilitator service verifies that a submitted payment payload satisfies the selected requirement and connects that
verification step to settlement.

Depending on the scheme, the facilitator may validate signatures, check expiration windows, ensure the selected asset
and network match the requirement, prevent replay, submit a transfer, or confirm that settlement has already occurred.
The merchant can then use the facilitator's result as the decision point for resource delivery.

This keeps the merchant application focused on pricing, access control, and resource delivery while delegating payment
verification and settlement mechanics to a service designed for that purpose.

## Transport Agnosticism

x402 payloads are transport-agnostic. The protocol units do not require HTTP semantics even though HTTP
`402 Payment Required` is the most obvious deployment shape.

A merchant can require payment and a client can send a payment payload over HTTP, a socket, Bluetooth, an RPC session, a
message queue, a local device channel, or any number of other transports. The transport only needs to carry the
requirement from merchant to client and the payload from client back to merchant or facilitator.

This means the lifecycle is the same whether the exchange looks like a `402` response followed by a retried HTTP
request, a socket message followed by a signed reply, or a nearby-device interaction where the requirement and payload
are exchanged without an HTTP server at all.

## Resource Delivery

The merchant can decide when to deliver the paid resource. It could for example fork a settlement fiber and eagerly
deliver the resource. Or it could block on settlement, and only deliver the resource upon confirmation.

Delivery may happen as the response to the retried request, as a socket message, as an out-of-band fulfillment event, or
through any other channel the program uses. The delivery step is not what defines x402. The x402 lifecycle is the
portable exchange of a requirement, a payload, and settlement verification that lets the merchant decide when delivery
is authorized.
